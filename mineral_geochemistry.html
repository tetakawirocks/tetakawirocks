Pourbaix Diagrams (Eh-pH)

Pourbaix diagram for the Fe-H₂O system showing iron species stability fields as a function of redox potential (Eh) and pH

Pourbaix diagrams, also known as Eh-pH diagrams, are fundamental tools in geochemistry and corrosion science. These diagrams map the stability fields of different chemical species as a function of redox potential (Eh) and pH, providing crucial insights into mineral stability and aqueous geochemistry.

Fundamental Concepts:

Eh (Redox Potential): Measured in volts, represents the tendency of a species to gain electrons (reduce) or lose electrons (oxidize). Positive Eh indicates oxidizing conditions, negative Eh indicates reducing conditions.

pH: Measure of hydrogen ion concentration, ranging from acidic (low pH) to alkaline (high pH) conditions.

Stability Fields: Regions where specific aqueous species or minerals are thermodynamically stable.

Construction Methodology:

Half-cell Reactions: Reactions are written with electrons (e⁻) on the left for reduction or right for oxidation

Nernst Equation: E = E° - (RT/nF)ln(Q) - Relates cell potential to reaction quotient

Gibbs Free Energy: ΔG° = -nFE° - Used to calculate standard potentials from thermodynamic data

Mass Action Law: K = [products]/[reactants] - Defines equilibrium constants

Key Boundary Lines:

O₂/H₂O Line: Upper boundary separating oxidizing from reducing conditions (O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O)

H⁺/H₂ Line: Lower boundary at Eh = 0 - (pH dependent): 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂

Species-Dependent Lines: Lines where iron species transform (Fe²⁺ ↔ Fe³⁺, Fe ↔ Fe²⁺, precipitation reactions)

Iron System Example:

Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺: Fe³⁺ + e⁻ → Fe²⁺ (depends on Eh only, vertical line)

Fe/Fe²⁺: Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Fe (metal dissolution)

Fe(OH)₃ precipitation: Fe³⁺ + 3H₂O ↔ Fe(OH)₃ + 3H⁺ (pH-dependent)

Fe²⁺ stability: Extends into reducing conditions at neutral to alkaline pH

Applications: Corrosion prediction, mineral processing, environmental geochemistry, water-rock interactions, and ore deposit genesis.

1

Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ ⇌ Fe(s) - Pure Redox Reaction

This exercise demonstrates how to construct a stability line for the reduction of ferrous iron to metallic iron. This reaction is pH-independent because no hydrogen ions are involved in the balanced equation.

Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ ⇌ Fe(s)
Step 1: Write the Half-Cell Reaction
The half-cell reaction is written with electrons on the left side for reduction:
Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Fe(s)
Step 2: Apply the Nernst Equation
Eh = E° - (0.05916/n) × log(Q)

Where Q = 1/[Fe²⁺] since Fe(s) has unit activity. At standard conditions [Fe²⁺] = 1 M, so log(Q) = 0.

Eh = E° - (0.05916/2) × log(1/[Fe²⁺])
Step 3: Final Stability Line Equation
Eh = -0.44 V - 0.0296 × log(1/[Fe²⁺])

This is a horizontal line on the Eh-pH diagram because the reaction does not depend on pH.
Practical Example

Problem: Calculate the Eh for a solution with [Fe²⁺] = 10⁻⁶ M

Eh = -0.44 - (0.0296) × log(1/10⁻⁶)
Eh = -0.44 - (0.0296) × 6
Eh = -0.617 V

This means metallic iron is stable below this Eh value when [Fe²⁺] = 10⁻⁶ M.

2

Fe³⁺ + H₂O ⇌ FeOH²⁺ + H⁺ - Hydrolysis Reaction

This exercise shows how hydrolysis reactions create pH-dependent stability lines. When H⁺ ions appear in the balanced equation, the stability field boundary will be a vertical line on the Eh-pH diagram.

Fe³⁺ + H₂O ⇌ FeOH²⁺ + H⁺
Step 1: Balance the Reaction
Fe³⁺ + H₂O → FeOH²⁺ + H⁺

Note that FeOH²⁺ has H⁺ ions "summed" on the right side of the balanced equation, which means this species will have a higher pH stability field.
Step 2: Apply the Nernst Equation
Eh = E° - (0.05916/n) × log([FeOH²⁺][H⁺]/[Fe³⁺])

This reaction does not involve electrons (n = 0), so it's a pure pH-dependent reaction.
Step 3: Final pH-Dependent Equation
pH = pK + log([Fe³⁺]/[FeOH²⁺])

This produces a vertical line on the Eh-pH diagram, representing the pH where Fe³⁺ and FeOH²⁺ are in equilibrium.
Practical Example

Problem: Calculate the pH where [Fe³⁺] = [FeOH²⁺]

pH = pK + log(1)
pH = pK

If K = 10⁻³·⁹⁴, then pH = 3.94. Below this pH, Fe³⁺ dominates; above this pH, FeOH²⁺ dominates.

3

Fe(OH)₃ + 3H⁺ ⇌ Fe³⁺ + 3H₂O - Solubility Equilibrium

This exercise demonstrates how to use Gibbs Free Energy to calculate equilibrium constants for precipitation-dissolution reactions. These reactions control iron solubility in natural waters.

Fe(OH)₃(s) + 3H⁺ ⇌ Fe³⁺ + 3H₂O
Step 1: Apply Gibbs Free Energy
ΔG° = ΣΔG°(products) - ΣΔG°(reactants)

ΔG° = [ΔG°(Fe³⁺) + 3×ΔG°(H₂O)] - [ΔG°(Fe(OH)₃) + 3×ΔG°(H⁺)]

ΔG° = -4.72 kJ/mol (using standard values)
Step 2: Calculate Standard Potential
Using ΔG° = -nFE°

For this reaction (no electrons transferred, n = 0), we calculate the equilibrium constant instead:

log K = -ΔG°/(2.303RT)
Step 3: Final Solubility Equation
pH = (1/3) × log([Fe³⁺]) + pK/3

This creates a line with slope = -1/3 on the Eh-pH diagram.
Practical Example

Problem: Calculate the pH where Fe(OH)₃ dissolves in a solution with [Fe³⁺] = 10⁻⁶ M

pH = (1/3) × log(10⁻⁶) + 2.0
pH = (1/3) × (-6) + 2.0
pH = 0

This explains why Fe(OH)₃ dissolves in acidic conditions (low pH).

4

Fe³⁺ + e⁻ ⇌ Fe²⁺ - Electron Transfer Reaction

This exercise shows the classic Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ redox couple. This is a pure redox reaction that creates a vertical line on the Eh-pH diagram because it involves electrons but no hydrogen ions.

Fe³⁺ + e⁻ ⇌ Fe²⁺
Step 1: Write the Half-Cell Reaction
Fe³⁺ + e⁻ → Fe²⁺

n = 1 (one electron transferred)
Step 2: Apply the Nernst Equation
Eh = E° - (0.05916/1) × log([Fe²⁺]/[Fe³⁺])

Eh = E° - 0.05916 × log([Fe²⁺]/[Fe³⁺])
Step 3: Final Redox Equation
Eh = 0.77 V - 0.05916 × log([Fe²⁺]/[Fe³⁺])

This is a vertical line at Eh = 0.77 V when [Fe²⁺] = [Fe³⁺].
Key Observation
The species with electrons on the left side of the balanced equation (Fe³⁺) is the species with the higher Eh value. This species dominates under oxidizing conditions.
Practical Example

Problem: Calculate the Eh for a solution where [Fe³⁺] = 10 × [Fe²⁺]

Eh = 0.77 - 0.05916 × log(0.1)
Eh = 0.77 - 0.05916 × (-1)
Eh = 0.83 V

Higher [Fe³⁺] pushes the equilibrium toward more oxidizing conditions.

5

Fe²⁺ + H₂O ⇌ FeOH⁺ + H⁺ - Ferrous Hydrolysis

This exercise demonstrates hydrolysis of ferrous iron (Fe²⁺), which is important for understanding iron mobility in reducing environments.

Fe²⁺ + H₂O ⇌ FeOH⁺ + H⁺
Step 1: Balance the Reaction
Fe²⁺ + H₂O → FeOH⁺ + H⁺

This is a hydrolysis reaction where water dissociates to form hydroxide complexes.
Step 2: Apply Mass Action Law
K = [FeOH⁺][H⁺] / [Fe²⁺]

Taking -log of both sides:

pH = pK + log([Fe²⁺]/[FeOH⁺])
Step 3: Stability Boundary
This produces a vertical line on the Eh-pH diagram at pH = pK, typically around pH 9.5 for the Fe²⁺/FeOH⁺ couple.
Practical Example

Problem: Calculate pH where [Fe²⁺] = [FeOH⁺]

pH = 9.5 + log(1)
pH = 9.5

Below pH 9.5, Fe²⁺ dominates; above pH 9.5, FeOH⁺ dominates.

6

Fe(OH)₃ + H⁺ + e⁻ ⇌ Fe²⁺ + 2H₂O - Redox-Solubility Control

This exercise combines both redox and pH effects, creating a diagonal line on the Eh-pH diagram. This is one of the most important boundaries in the iron system.

Fe(OH)₃(s) + H⁺ + e⁻ ⇌ Fe²⁺ + 2H₂O
Step 1: Combine Half-Cell and Hydrolysis
This reaction combines the precipitation of Fe(OH)₃ with the reduction of Fe³⁺ to Fe²⁺.

n = 1 (one electron transferred)
Step 2: Apply the Nernst Equation
Eh = E° - 0.05916 × log([Fe²⁺]/[H⁺]²)

Rearranging to solve for Fe²⁺:

log([Fe²⁺]) = 2pH - (Eh/0.05916) + constant
Step 3: Final Diagonal Equation
Eh = -0.6 + 0.177 × pH - 0.05916 × log([Fe²⁺])

This creates a line with positive slope on the Eh-pH diagram.
Practical Example

Problem: Calculate Eh at pH 7 with [Fe²⁺] = 10⁻⁶ M

Eh = -0.6 + 0.177(7) - 0.05916 × log(10⁻⁶)
Eh = -0.6 + 1.24 - 0.05916 × (-6)
Eh = 1.51 V

This high value indicates Fe(OH)₃ is stable under these conditions.

7

Complete Eh-pH Diagram for Iron - Interpreting Stability Fields

This final exercise shows how all seven boundaries combine to create the complete Pourbaix diagram for the Fe-H₂O system at 1M concentration.

Complete System: Fe(s) ⇌ Fe²⁺ ⇌ Fe³⁺ ⇌ Fe(OH)₃(s) ⇌ Fe(OH)₄⁻
Step 1: Identify All Boundary Lines
The diagram contains 7 major boundaries:

1. O₂/H₂O Line: Upper boundary (oxidizing conditions)
2. H⁺/H₂ Line: Lower boundary (reducing conditions)
3. Fe/Fe²⁺: Horizontal at Eh = -0.44 V
4. Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺: Vertical at Eh = 0.77 V
5. Fe(OH)₃ solubility: Diagonal lines
6. Hydrolysis boundaries: Vertical lines
Step 2: Interpret Stability Regions
Above O₂/H₂O: Oxidizing conditions, H₂O decomposes to O₂
Between O₂/H₂O and Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺: Fe³⁺ stable (oxidizing)
Between Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ and H⁺/H₂: Fe²⁺ stable (mildly reducing)
Below H⁺/H₂: Strongly reducing, H₂ gas evolution
Important Observations
Species with electrons on the left side of the balanced equation (Fe³⁺, Fe(OH)₃ with reduction) dominate at higher Eh values. Species appearing on the right (Fe²⁺, Fe) dominate at lower Eh values. Hydrolysis products appear at higher pH values.
Practical Example - Natural Water Analysis

Problem: A groundwater sample has pH = 6.5 and Eh = -0.2 V. Determine the dominant iron species.

Step 1: Check O₂/H₂O line at pH 6.5
Eh = 1.23 - 0.059 × 6.5 = 0.85 V (sample is below)

Step 2: Check H⁺/H₂ line at pH 6.5
Eh = 0 - 0.059 × 6.5 = -0.38 V (sample is above)

Step 3: Check Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ at Eh = -0.2 V
The sample Eh (-0.2 V) is below 0.77 V, so Fe²⁺ dominates

Answer: Fe²⁺ (ferrous iron) is the dominant species in these reducing conditions.
