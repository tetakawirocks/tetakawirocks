ASTM D388 Classification

The ASTM D388 standard provides a systematic classification of coals by rank, based on the progressive carbonification (coalification) process. This classification uses Fixed Carbon (FC) and Volatile Matter (VM) content as primary parameters.

Classification Parameters:

  • Fixed Carbon: Residual combustible carbon after volatile matter is expelled
  • Volatile Matter: Compounds released as gas or vapor during heating
  • Moisture and Ash: Non-combustible components affecting coal quality
  • Reflectance (Ro): Vitrinite reflectance for maturity determination

Rank Progression: From Lignite through Subbituminous, Bituminous, to Anthracite, reflecting increasing carbon content and thermal maturity.

Ternary diagram of maceral composition showing Vitrinite, Liptinite, and Inertinite proportions

Maceral Composition Ternary

Ternary diagrams are essential tools for visualizing the relative proportions of three components that sum to 100%. For coal, the maceral ternary plot displays the composition of the three main organic components.

Maceral Groups:

  • Vitrinite: Gelified vegetable tissue, dominant in most coals (58-70%), principal source of hydrocarbons
  • Liptinite: Resistant plant material (spores, cuticles, resin), high H/C ratio, precursor of liquid hydrocarbons
  • Inertinite: Oxidized or carbonized material, low hydrocarbon potential, evidence of fire or oxidation

Applications: Organic matter typing, depositional environment interpretation, and hydrocarbon potential assessment.

Diessel facies diagram showing GWI vs TPI indices for depositional environment interpretation

Diessel Facies Indices

The Diessel facies indices (GWI and TPI) utilize maceral composition to determine the depositional environment of coal measures. These indices provide quantitative tools for environmental interpretation.

Facies Indices:

  • GWI (Gelification Index): GWI = (Vitrinite + Semifusinite) / (Inertinite + Liptinite) - Measures wet vs. dry conditions
  • TPI (Tissue Preservation Index): TPI = (Telinite + Collinita) / (Vitrinite + Inertodetrina) - Indicates tissue preservation quality

Environmental Interpretation: High GWI and TPI values indicate paralic (coastal) environments with abundant vegetation and good preservation conditions.

Radar plot showing major oxide composition (SiO₂, Al₂O₃, Fe₂O₃, CaO, MgO, and others)

Major Oxide Radar Diagram

Radar diagrams (spider plots) display multi-variable data patterns radially, allowing for rapid visual comparison of samples across multiple parameters simultaneously.

Major Oxides Analyzed:

  • SiO₂ (40-60%): Siliciclastic mineralogy indicator
  • Al₂O₃ (15-30%): Clay mineral content
  • Fe₂O₃ (5-20%): Sulfide and carbonate presence
  • CaO (1-15%): Carbonate and gypsum content
  • MgO, Na₂O, K₂O, TiO₂: Additional mineralogical indicators

Applications: Mineralogical characterization, ash behavior prediction, and source rock identification.

Van Krevelen diagram showing H/C vs O/C atomic ratios for kerogen typing and thermal maturity

Van Krevelen Diagram

The Van Krevelen diagram is a fundamental tool for visualizing the evolution of organic matter during thermal maturation. It plots hydrogen-to-carbon (H/C) ratios against oxygen-to-carbon (O/C) ratios.

Atomic Ratio Calculations:

  • H/C = (H% / 1.008) / (C% / 12.011)
  • O/C = (O% / 16.00) / (C% / 12.011)

Kerogen Types:

  • Type I: Algal kerogen, high H/C (1.5-1.8), oil-prone
  • Type II: Marine kerogen, moderate H/C (1.2-1.5), oil and gas
  • Type III: Herbaceous kerogen, lower H/C (0.8-1.2), gas-prone
Thermal maturation trends showing C, H, O elemental evolution from Lignite to Anthracite

Thermal Maturation Trends

Thermal maturation (coalification) trends illustrate how elemental composition changes during burial and heating. These diagrams show the progressive increase in carbon content and the loss of oxygen and hydrogen.

Observed Trends:

  • Carbon (C): Progressive increase from ~65% to ~95%
  • Hydrogen (H): Slight decrease from ~5.5% to ~2.5%
  • Oxygen (O): Significant decrease from ~30% to ~1.5%

Process: Thermal maturation involves selective loss of oxygen-containing functional groups, progressive aromatization, and carbon concentration.

Bar chart showing atomic ratios (H/C, O/C, N/C, S/C) for geochemical characterization

Atomic Ratio Analysis

Atomic ratio bar charts provide a visual comparison of different elemental ratios normalized to carbon. These ratios reveal information about the molecular structure of organic matter.

Key Atomic Ratios:

  • H/C > 1.0: Hydrogen-rich organic matter (lipidic)
  • H/C < 0.8: Aromatized organic matter (high rank)
  • O/C: Oxygen functional group indicator
  • N/C: Nitrogenous compound relationships
  • S/C: Organic sulfur in heterocyclic structures

Interpretation: Ratios provide fingerprints of organic matter type, thermal maturity, and depositional conditions.

Chondrite-normalized REE patterns showing LREE enrichment and negative Eu anomaly

REE Normalization Patterns

Rare Earth Element (REE) normalization eliminates the Oddo-Harkins effect, revealing true fractionation patterns. Normalization to Chondrite (McDonough & Sun, 1995) allows comparison across samples and depositional systems.

REE Classification:

  • LREE (Light): La, Ce, Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu - typically enriched in felsic sources
  • HREE (Heavy): Gd, Tb, Dy, Ho, Er, Tm, Yb, Lu - more compatible in mafic systems

Key Anomalies:

  • Eu anomaly: Eu / √(Sm × Gd) - plagioclase fractionation indicator
  • Ce anomaly: Ce / √(La × Pr) - redox condition proxy
  • LREE/HREE: Source rock composition fingerprint
Scatter plot of Fixed Carbon vs Volatile Matter showing coal rank classification zones

Fixed Carbon vs Volatile Matter

The Fixed Carbon vs Volatile Matter cross-plot is a fundamental visualization for coal rank classification. Samples plot in distinct zones corresponding to their thermal maturity.

Rank Zones:

  • Lignite: Low FC (<50%), high VM (>40%), low maturity
  • Subbituminous: Intermediate FC (50-65%), VM (30-40%)
  • Bituminous: High FC (65-92%), low VM (8-31%), primary coal for energy
  • Anthracite: Very high FC (>92%), very low VM (<4%), highest rank

Applications: Quality assessment, industrial application determination, and exploration target evaluation.
